## How the Product Works

The product has two verticals:

**1. Physical Network:**

- Hundreds of km of fiber connected directly to subsea cables
- Organized in loops where ISPs can plug in
- Once connected, ISP effectively operates as vertically integrated
- Share has computers interfacing directly with subsea cables, then fiber lines creating loops for regional ISPs

**2. Financial/SaaS Platform:**

- Revolutionary: no upfront capacity commits
- Share gives every partner "1,000 lane highways" regardless of size
- Takes a fee (percentage) from what partners collect from end users
- Platform = "ISP bank account" — balance, user payments, financial products
- Automated revenue split (e.g., 80% to partner, 20% to Share — actual percentages vary)
- First financial product: line of credit for partners to finance fiber, hardware, network expansion

**Luis's simplistic example:**

- ISP in Kenya with 500 users joins Share
- No longer pays for data upfront → frees capital for marketing, hardware, network growth
- Only financial interaction with Share is when users pay monthly bills through Share's portal
- Share takes fee, partner keeps the rest

**Stripe analogy:**

**Energy sector analogy:**
Peer-to-peer energy grid companies let households sell excess solar energy back to the grid. Share does the same for bandwidth — coordinating excess infrastructure capacity across a utility asset.

## Coordination Explained

- Internet = combination of dumb infrastructure (fiber) that moves data + intelligent nodes (routers, switches in data centers)
- Share places its own networking equipment ("little brains") in data centers and micro data centers, interconnecting with existing fiber from multiple providers
- Without Share: each ISP needs separate contracts with each infrastructure provider (60-page documents with SLAs each)
- With Share: ISPs connect to one entity (Share) and access all aggregated infrastructure

## Hyperscale — Precise Definition

From Notion kickoff doc: "The 'hyperscale' part of the model refers to how the network grows. Instead of building everything from scratch, Share aggregates existing underutilized infrastructure (dark fiber, idle assets) into one ecosystem. Existing infrastructure can plug into the Share network, which means coverage expands without Share having to lay every meter of cable."

Key distinction: Share does not build infrastructure — it activates infrastructure that already exists but is underutilized.

## Market Context

_Previously compiled — needs verification_

**Kenya's ISP Landscape:**
Kenya has a competitive and growing ISP market. Key players include Safaricom (dominant), Zuku, Faiba (Jamii Telecom), Airtel, Telkom Kenya, Poa Internet, Mawingu, and newer entrants like Vilcom. Starlink's entry in Kenya has also disrupted pricing. Approximately 1.5 million fixed data subscribers, market growing as providers expand fiber and wireless coverage.

**The Connectivity Gap:**
Over 50% of Africa's population still has no internet access. Five of the 10 most expensive countries for mobile data are in sub-Saharan Africa. Small ISPs struggle with high upfront bandwidth costs, limited infrastructure access, and lack of management tools. Share's zero-commitment model directly addresses these barriers.

**Infrastructure Reality:**
Kenya has 5+ submarine cables landing (SEACOM, EASSy, LION2, DARE 1, and others), government fiber backbone projects (NOFBI), and growing data center investment. The infrastructure exists but is unevenly distributed and often underutilized — which is exactly what Share aggregates.
