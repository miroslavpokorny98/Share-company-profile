## Homepage Storyboard Walkthrough (what VKZ presented)

VKZ structured the homepage as a single scroll-based animation in three phases:

1. **Problem** — Africa has the infrastructure (subsea cables landed, towers up, fiber everywhere) but it's not connected. Cables run in parallel, never together. Visual: a fiber cable with one core lit, the rest unconnected — metaphor for unrealized potential.
2. **Solution** — Share comes in as the coordination layer Africa was missing. USPs surface (unlimited backhaul, no upfront commits).
3. **Vision** — zoom out to all of Africa connected in the Share ecosystem.

After the animation, the homepage continues with a credibility section (numbers, partner logos, testimonials) and CTAs leading to separate ISP and user journeys.

The storyboard frames don't yet animate into each other — that will happen in the final build. Visual is intentionally bare (no textures, just 3D objects communicating concept).

## Narrative Structure Feedback

### Hero must stand alone

**Luis:** light-touch introduction to what Share is at the very top, *before* the problem/solution story begins. Users who don't scroll still need to understand what they're looking at.

**Jose, going further:** something stronger than "light" — a complete, clear definition of Share in its essence right at the hero, then the immersive scroll explains the *why*.

> *"Some people just do not scroll. It's the reality. Somebody might see it on mobile. Some people might not even have a lot of data, so the scrolling experience might be a little like an overkill. When I open the website, what needs to appear there is a complete, and I wouldn't even say light, I would say a complete definition of who we are in its essence. Like a very clear, even if it's not with a lot of info that are very clear, share is this, boom, very clear. Now I get into a submersive experience in which I am maybe taking into the value of the why."* — Jose

### Tone correction — no outsider narrative

Jose flagged that framing Africa as "missing" something feels like an outsider narrative — Share's positioning should come from within, as an enabler activating what's already there.

> *"To say that Africa is missing something and that we're bringing it hurts us more than in a way, because I feel it is the stereotypical narrative that somebody from not here would come and kind of impose. What really works really well with us... is that we kind of go all the way into becoming one more. Really this is a value presentation that comes from the fact that we're joining companies that are global. So that it almost comes from within. We're just powering, activating, creating an exponential value of what's already valuable."* — Jose

### Heavy infrastructure framing — for investors

Share wants to be perceived as heavy infrastructure (not just SaaS). Jose: 80% of their CapEx is infra. In the current VC environment, hardware is a real differentiator that AI can't replicate.

> *"Everybody's building SaaS right now. Everybody is building, especially now with AI, if you're just a company that you only have software, you have very little competitive advantage... every single company that is in the Y Combinator this year got cloned in 24 hours by one agent. That's the world in which we live in right now. What is the differentiation in companies right now? It's about people. It's about the relationships you make on the human level outside of the solution. And who are the most valuable companies today that feel that are very competitive in the VC world? Those that have hardware. Because AI does not touch that. It can't replicate it. It's actual physical silicon. It's fiber optic cables that connect."* — Jose

## Visualizing the Coordination Layer

Core insight: the current storyboard reads as a fiber company — Luis, Jose, and (implicitly via Omar's earlier work) the team agreed the visual needs to show Share as the thing that **coordinates all infrastructure types** (cables, towers, data centers, and potentially satellites), not just fiber.

**Omar's fungi metaphor** (raised mid-call by Luis on Omar's behalf): like the Tokyo subway experiment where fungi find the most efficient path and spread as a living organism — Share touches and activates any hardware it connects to.

> *"It's kind of like a fungi. If you guys have ever seen the video of how they designed the subway station in Tokyo, where they deployed a fungi to figure out the most efficient path. They just run this experiment and you could see just the fungi take over the different alleys and connect all of them like an organism, like a living organism. Because that gives the idea of like, yeah, we're not just cable. We're not data centers. We're not towers. We are the thing that makes everything in unison, regardless of what type of hardware. We could even, I'm going to say it out there, and maybe we didn't even do it, but it could even be that satellite. We actually work with satellite."* — Luis

### Two visual options proposed by VKZ

Miroslav summarized two options for the coordination-layer visual:

1. **Optical cable cross-section** showing multiple cores, most unlit/unused, with Share activating all of them — metaphor for unrealized potential.
2. **Top-down map view** with interconnecting nodes, closer to what the internal dashboard shows.

**Luis's synthesis:** keep the cable storyline for the problem/solution section, but add the coordination layer concept *at the top* as the identity-establishing visual. The cable explains "what's the problem and how we solve it"; the coordination explains "who we are and what we do."

## Fiber Cable Anatomy (technical detail from Jose & Omar)

This came up because the storyboard shows a single fiber core already lit, which to an industry viewer reads as "the splice has already happened" — i.e., the problem already solved.

- **Outer cable contains ~96 cores** (color-coded tubes).
- **Each tube holds 12 fibers**, also color-coded.
- **Most companies activate only ~5 of those cores.** That's a big chunk of Share's value: coordinating and activating the rest.
- **Splicing process:** two fiber ends placed in a machine that melts the glass together into one continuous fiber — the physical act of connection.
- Color coding is for identification (e.g., "the red cable in the blue tube"), not aesthetic.

> *"What everybody understands... is that most companies, they have a big cable industry, but out of the 96, they only have activated five of them. That's a big value proposition of what we're doing of coordination, because the rest are waste."* — Jose

**Luis's brand caution:** the actual color coding uses bright colors (red, blue, green) — wants to make sure that doesn't dilute the brand. *"This is also just very tacky, the way that the actual colors go. But if we do have these very bright colors, the brand could get a little bit diluted."*

**Action confirmed by Miroslav:** the final cable visual will show multiple fiber lines, with only some connected, matching the actual structure of a fiber cable.

## Neutrality vs. Control — Messaging Tension

Miroslav flagged a messaging tension between the two audiences:

- ISPs need to feel Share has infrastructure and control.
- Infra suppliers need to feel their assets stay theirs.

**Luis's resolution:** both should feel "enablement and accessibility," not control. A supplier today could become an infra provider to the ecosystem tomorrow. Use phrase: *"neutral infrastructure provider."*

> *"We're not almost coming to this 'Hey, I'm gonna control your infrastructure'. I am an enabler. I am a neutral infrastructure provider, something that we're using lately. The fact that we're neutral, that we're enablers, that we're an ecosystem creator is very important in maybe the language and the way that we communicate so that the person that is looking at this and is an infra provider says, 'oh, I want my infra that is actually underutilized... I want to put it in here.' As opposed to maybe 'who's this company that's going to control infrastructure, my hard work?'"* — Luis

> *"Even the small provider has a network of its own with cables. And it is very possible that in the future, if that network is growing, they might also become an infrastructure provider to the ecosystem. They might say, 'hey, I have cables that I'm not actually using as much. I would like for me to put this into the ecosystem.'"* — Luis

**Important nuance:** even though framing is neutrality/enablement, suppliers actually *want* to feel that Share has hardware. *"We very much feel comfortable with them feeling that we have infra because it is what builds the trust for them. We're kind of the glue of both."*

## PNG Sequence vs. Real-Time 3D Rendering — DECIDED: Real-Time

VKZ presented both options:

**PNG sequence:**
- Designers render the animation to video / image sequence; developer optimizes and embeds it.
- No interactivity on hover or pause.
- Page weighs **30+ MB** (optimal is 2–3 MB) — breaks on slow connections / mobile.

**Real-time 3D rendering (WebGL):**
- Geometry and movement handled in the browser at runtime.
- Interactive, works well on mobile, loads efficiently.
- VKZ's previous projects (Lightweight) use this approach.

**Decision: real-time WebGL.** Jose was direct:

> *"We as a company are judged by two things — our pretty faces in in-person meetings and the quality of our network. So if we have a website that doesn't load... because it's literally what we do, it's internet. So we do have a very clear option, which is for it to work well."* — Jose

Miroslav and Thao both strongly recommended real-time given the scroll length and the internet-speed constraints of Share's audience in Africa.
