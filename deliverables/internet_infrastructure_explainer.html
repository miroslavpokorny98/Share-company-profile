Layer 01

Subsea Cables

The internet is physical. At the bottom of every ocean, there are cables — about as thick as a garden hose — carrying almost all international internet traffic.

A single cable contains multiple fiber pairs — each pair can carry terabits per second of data

A subsea cable is essentially a bundle of glass threads (fiber optics) wrapped in layers of steel, copper, and plastic — roughly the diameter of a garden hose, sitting on the ocean floor for thousands of kilometres. Light pulses travel through the glass at near light-speed, carrying internet data between continents.

They're built and co-owned by consortiums of telecoms companies. Capacity on the cable is sold in "pairs" or wavelengths — specific lanes of the highway that different companies purchase rights to use.

East Africa's cables

SEACOM (2009), EASSy (2010), TEAMS (2009), LION2, and DARE1 (2023) all land in Mombasa, Kenya. More are coming — 2Africa (Meta-backed) will be the longest ever when complete. Each cable dramatically increases available capacity to the region.

Why it matters

Before these cables existed, East Africa relied on satellite — expensive, slow, and high-latency. Each new subsea cable landing drops the price of bandwidth significantly. There is now theoretically enough cable capacity in East Africa to connect everyone. The problem is getting it inland.

Simple analogy

Think of subsea cables as the transatlantic motorways. Enormous capacity, shared by many, laid once and used for decades. The problem isn't the motorway — it's the local roads that connect people to them.

Layer 02 – 03

Landing Stations & Backbone Fiber

Where the ocean cable meets land, and how data then travels hundreds of kilometres inland to reach cities.

Multiple competing fiber operators build parallel routes between the same cities — duplicated infrastructure, not shared

Landing station: The physical building at the coast where the undersea cable comes ashore. Here the marine cable connects to terrestrial fiber. It's a heavily secured facility with massive power backup systems and specialist marine communications equipment. Mombasa is East Africa's primary landing point.

Backbone fiber: From the landing station, data travels inland along fiber-optic cables buried underground or strung on poles. These backbone routes connect coastal landing stations to major inland cities (Nairobi, Kampala, Dar es Salaam). Companies like Liquid Intelligent Technologies, Safaricom, and government-owned NOFBI own these inland fiber routes.

The fragmentation problem

The same routes (e.g., Mombasa → Nairobi) are served by 5+ competing fiber operators, each with their own cables in the ground. They don't share. ISPs must sign separate contracts with each one — meaning if you want redundancy, you need multiple 60-page agreements, each with its own pricing and SLA. The infrastructure is duplicated; the access is fragmented.

Layer 04

Data Centers & Internet Exchange Points

Where all the fibers from different providers physically meet and interconnect — the internet's nervous system.

A data center is where multiple fiber networks physically terminate and interconnect. Whoever has equipment here can connect to everyone else.

A data center is a large, highly secure building full of server racks — cabinets packed with networking equipment (routers and switches) that direct internet traffic. They have industrial cooling systems, multiple power generators, and physical security because if the equipment fails, thousands of networks go offline.

The key function is interconnection. Because all the major fiber providers, backbone operators, and ISPs plug their cables into the same building, data can be handed off between networks. Without data centers acting as meeting points, networks would be isolated islands.

Simple analogy

A data center is like an airport. Airlines (fiber networks) land their planes (data) there, passengers (data packets) transfer between flights, and they continue to their destination. Without airports as common meeting points, every airline would need its own dedicated runway to every city.

Layer 05

Local ISPs — The Last Mile

The companies who actually connect homes and businesses to the internet. They buy access to the backbone and resell it to end users.

An ISP buys capacity from the backbone, then resells it to end users. Their cost is fixed; their revenue depends on how many subscribers they can acquire.

A local ISP is the company that actually connects homes and businesses to the internet. They install the physical connection — whether that's a fiber cable run to your building, a wireless antenna on your roof, or a 4G/LTE router. They give you a router, an account, and a monthly bill.

To do this, they first need to buy internet access from the backbone operators. This is called IP transit or backhaul. They commit to renting a certain amount of capacity (e.g., 1 Gbps) per month — whether their subscribers use it all or not. In East Africa, this committed capacity is expensive: a small ISP might pay $5,000–$20,000/month to the backbone just to have internet to resell.

What "backhaul" means

Backhaul is the connection between the ISP and the backbone. It's the "back" part of the chain — invisible to end users but the ISP's biggest cost and most critical dependency. If backhaul is expensive, slow, or unreliable, the ISP's service suffers.

What "commit" means

ISPs don't pay per gigabyte used — they pay for a committed pipe size (e.g., 500 Mbps). Like renting 2 lanes of motorway whether you use them or not. If a football match airs and traffic spikes, they need to have bought enough lanes in advance or users experience slow internet.

  • 1
    ISP signs a capacity agreement with a backbone operator. Commits to paying for X Mbps per month for 1-3 years.
  • 2
    ISP builds their local network — installs routers, runs fiber to buildings, sets up antennas. Requires capital outlay before a single subscriber is connected.
  • 3
    ISP acquires subscribers — door to door, building by building. Each subscriber pays a monthly fee (e.g., 1,300–4,000 Ksh in Kenya).
  • 4
    ISP collects payments, pays backbone, keeps margin. If they have 100 subscribers but committed for 500, they're paying for capacity they're not using — cash flow problem.
Why ISPs stay small

Growth requires capital (to build network). Capital comes from subscribers. Subscribers only come once network is built. Meanwhile, the monthly backbone commit is a fixed overhead whether you're growing or not. This catch-22 keeps small ISPs small — they can't afford to take the financial risk of expanding to new areas.

The Problem

Why 600 Million People
Aren't Connected

It's not because the infrastructure doesn't exist. It's because the infrastructure isn't coordinated.

Share does the hard work of contracting all the backbone providers — ISPs only need to deal with Share

The cables are there. The fiber is (mostly) there. The data centers exist. But the system is economically and contractually fragmented in ways that make it nearly impossible for small ISPs to grow — and therefore nearly impossible for millions of people to get online.

Problem 1: Cost stacking

Every layer in the chain marks up the cost. Subsea cable capacity → backbone transit → ISP → user. Each layer adds fees. By the time internet reaches a user in rural Kenya, it's been marked up multiple times over what it cost at the cable.

Problem 2: Upfront capital

ISPs must commit to paying for bandwidth capacity months or years in advance, whether or not they have enough subscribers to justify it. This financial risk stops small ISPs from expanding to underserved areas where demand exists but isn't yet monetised.

Problem 3: Duplicated infrastructure, zero coordination

Five fiber companies may have cables running down the same street in Nairobi — each one serving a handful of ISPs, none of them talking to each other. Meanwhile, 50km away there's no fiber at all. The issue isn't lack of infrastructure; it's that infrastructure is built competitively and not shared. Share describes this as "5 Tesla highways, 5 Ford highways, 5 Volkswagen highways — instead of one shared road."

Where Share Fits

Share's Role in the Chain

Share inserts itself between the backbone infrastructure and local ISPs — becoming the single coordination layer that no one else has built.

Share sits between the backbone infrastructure and local ISPs — doing all the aggregation work so ISPs don't have to

What Share actually does

The coordination layer nobody else built

Share has already signed agreements with all the major backbone providers and subsea cable operators. When a local ISP joins Share, they get access to all of that aggregated infrastructure through a single connection — no 60-page contracts, no upfront capacity commits, no capital risk.

Physical layer

The Network

Share places its own networking equipment in data centers and micro data centers across Kenya, interconnecting with existing fiber from multiple providers. ISPs plug into Share's PoP (Point of Presence) in their area and instantly gain access to all of Share's aggregated bandwidth — connected directly to 7 subsea cables and Tier 1 providers.

Financial layer

The Platform

Share flips the payment model. Instead of ISPs paying upfront, Share gives them unlimited bandwidth and takes a percentage of what they collect from end users — paid automatically through Share's billing portal. Share becomes the ISP's bank: showing their balance, managing payments, and offering financial products like lines of credit to fund network expansion.

The Stripe parallel

Stripe didn't ask e-commerce shops to build their own payment infrastructure or sign deals with Visa, Mastercard, and every bank separately. They said: add this module to your website, we handle everything else, we take a small percentage. Share is the same for ISPs with internet infrastructure. It's an add-on, not a replacement of what the ISP already does.
